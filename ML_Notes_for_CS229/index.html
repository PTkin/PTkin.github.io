ML Notes for CS229

Posted on 2016-06-09

This is just a note from PTkin, who is learning CS229 of Stanford University.

Basic Concept

  • features
  • target
  • training example
  • training set
  • hypothesis
  • classification
  • regression
  • iteration
  • supervised learning
  • unsupervised learning

Linear Regression

  • whete $ \theta_i $ are the parameters (also called weithts):

    $$ h_\theta(x) = {\theta_0} + {\theta_1 x_1} + {\theta_2 x_2} $$

  • To simplify our notation, we also introduce the convention of letting $ x_0 = 1 $ (this is the intercept term), so that our hypothesis is:

    $$ h(x) = \sum_{i=0}^n {\theta_i x_i} = \theta^T x $$

  • Cost Function:

    $$ J(\theta) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^m{(h_\theta(x^{(i)}) - y^{(i)})}^2 $$

  • goal:

    minimize cost function $ J(\theta) $

  • prediction:

    $$ h_\theta(x) = \theta^T x $$

  • ordinary least squares regression model

LMS algorithm

  • gradient descent algorithm:

    1. start with some initial $ \theta $
    2. repeatedly performs $ \theta_j := \theta_j - \alpha \frac{\partial{}}{\partial{\theta_j}} J(\theta) = \theta_j - \alpha (h_\theta(x^{(i)}) - y^{(i)}) x_j^{(i)} $ until convergence
    3. $ \alpha $ is called the learning rate

  • now let’s calculate $ \frac{\partial{}}{\partial{\theta_j}} J(\theta) $:

    $$ \begin{align} \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta_j} J(\theta) &= \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta_j} \frac{1}{2} {(h_\theta(x) - y)^2} \\ &= 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2}(h_\theta(x)-y) \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta_j} (h_\theta(x) - y) \\ &= (h_\theta(x) - y) \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta_j}(\sum_{i=0}^n \theta_i x_i - y) \\ &= (h_\theta(x) - y) x_j \\ \end{align} $$

  • so for a single training example, this gives the update rule:

    $$ \theta_j := \theta_j - \alpha (h_\theta(x^{(i)}) - y^{(i)}) x_j^{(i)} $$

  • The rule is called the LMS update rule (stands for “least mean squares”), and is also known as the Widrow-Hoff learning rule

  • There are two kinds of gradient descent:

    1. batch gradient descent

      • Each step of gradient descent uses all the training examples
      • can get the minimum of $ J(\theta) $
      • high-cost operation if the training set is large

    2. stochastic gradient descent (also incremental gradient descent)

      • repeatedly run through the training set, and each time we encounter a training example, we update the parameters according to the gradient of the error with respect to that single training example only
      • get the $ \theta $ where $ J(\theta) $ be close to the minimum
      • faster

The normal equations

Normal equation: Method to solve for $ \theta $ analytically.

  1. define the design matrix X to be the m-by-n matrix (actually m-by-n + 1, if we include the intercept term) that contains the training examples’ input values in its rows:

    $$ X = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & {(x^{(1)})^T} \\ 1 & {(x^{(2)})^T} \\ \vdots & \vdots \\ 1 & {(x^{(m)})^T} \\ \end{bmatrix} $$

  2. then let $ \overrightarrow y $ be the m-dimensional vector containing all the target values from the training set:

    $$ \overrightarrow y = \begin{bmatrix} y^{(1)} \\ y^{(2)} \\ \vdots \\ y^{(m)} \\ \end{bmatrix} $$

  3. and now we give the conclusion directly:

    $$ \theta = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T y $$

then we’ll give the proving:

  1. At first we know $ X $、$ \theta $、$ \overrightarrow y $, so we can get

    $$ \begin{align} X \theta- \overrightarrow y &= \begin{bmatrix} (X^{(1)})^T \theta \\ \vdots \\ (X^{(m)})^T \theta \\ \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} y^{(1)} \\ \vdots \\ y^{(m)} \\ \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} h_\theta(x^{(1)}) - y^{(1)} \\ \vdots \\ h_\theta(x^{(m)}) - y^{(m)} \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{align} $$

  2. then using the fact that for a vector $ z $, we have that

    $$ z^T z = \sum_i{z_i^2} $$

  3. so

    $$ \begin{align} \frac{1}{2}(X \theta - \overrightarrow y)^T (X \theta - \overrightarrow y) &= \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^m(h_\theta(x^{(i)}) - y^{(i)})^2 \\ &= J(\theta) \\ \end{align} $$

  4. …

What if $ X^T X $ is non-invertible (singular/ degenerate)?

  1. Redundant features (linearly dependent).

  2. Too many features:

    • Delete some features, or use regularization.

Feature Scaling

Idea: Make sure features are on a similar scale. E.g.

  • $ x_1 $ = size (0-2000 feet2)
  • $ x_2 $ = number of bedrooms (1-5)

  1. Min-Max Normalization

    $$ x_i := \frac{(x_i - min)}{max - min} $$

  2. Mean Normalization

    $$ x_i := \frac{(x_i- \overline x)}{max - min} $$

  3. zero-mean normalization

    $$ x_i := \frac{(x_i - \overline x)}{std(x_i)} $$

Comparing Gradient Descent and Normal Equation

$ m $ training examples, $ n $ features.

  1. Gradient Descent

    • Need to choose $ \alpha $
    • Needs many iterations
    • Works well even when $ n $ is large.

  2. Normal Equation

    • No need to choose $ \alpha $
    • Don’t need to iterate
    • Need to compute $ (X^T X)^{-1} $
    • Slow if $ n $ is very large

Locally weighted linear regression

  • underfitting
  • overfitting
  • LWR: Locally weighted linear regression
  • parametric algorithm: has a fixed, finite number of parameters (the $ \theta^{(i)} $’s), which are fit to the data

  • non-parametric algorithm

  • In the original linear regression algorithm, we would

    Fit $ \theta $ to minimize $ \sum_i{(\theta^Tx^{(i)} - y^{(i)}) ^ 2} $

  • In contrast, the locally weighted linear regression algorithm does the following:

    • Fit $ \theta $ to minimize $ \sum_i{\omega^{(i)}(\theta^Tx^{(i)} - y^{(i)}) ^ 2} $
    • Here, the $ \omega^{(i)} $ are non-negative valued weights
    • A fairly standard choice for the weights is $ \omega^{(i)} = \exp(-\frac{(x^{(i)} - x)^2}{2\tau^2}) $
    • The parameter $ \tau $ controls how quickly the weight of a training example falls off with distance of its $ x^{(i)} $ from the query point x; τ is called the bandwidth parameter

Logistic Regression

  • binary classification
  • negative class
  • positive class
  • label

Logistic Regression

  • We choose $ g(z) $ called logistic function or the sigmoid function that:

    $$ g(z) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}} $$

  • so we have hypotheses $ h_\theta(x) $ that:

    $$ h_\theta(x) = g(\theta^T x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-\theta^T x}} $$

  • $ g(z) $ looks like this:

  • here’s a useful property of the derivative of the sigmoid function, which we write as $ g’(z) $:

    $$ \begin{align} g'(z) &= \frac{d}{dz} \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}} \\ &= \frac{1}{(1+e^{-z})^2} (e^{-z}) \\ &= \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}} \cdot (1 - \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}}) \\ &= g(z) (1 - g(z)) \\ \end{align} $$

  • Decision Boundary

  • Cost Function of Linear regression:

    $$ \begin{align} Cost(h_\theta(x^{(i)}, y^{(i)}) &= (h_\theta(x^{(i)}) - y^{(i)})^2 \\ J(\theta) &= \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^m Cost(h_\theta(x^{(i)}, y^{(i)}) \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^m{(h_\theta(x^{(i)}) - y^{(i)})}^2 \\ \end{align} $$

  • Cost Function of Logistic Regression:

    $$ \begin{align} Cost(h_\theta(x^{(i)}, y^{(i)}) &= \begin{cases} -\log(h_\theta(x)) , & \text{if y = 1} \\ -\log(1 - h_\theta(x)), & \text{if y = 0} \\ \end{cases} \\ J(\theta) &= \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m Cost(h_\theta(x^{(i)}, y^{(i)}) \\ &= -\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m \left[ y^{(i)} \log h_\theta(x^{(i)}) + (1 - y^{(i)}) \log (1 - h_\theta(x^{(i)})) \right] \\ \end{align} $$

  • goal:

    minimize cost function $ J(\theta) $

  • prediction:

    $$ h_\theta(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-\theta^T x}} $$

  • To minimize $ J(\theta) $, we have:

    $$ \begin{align} \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta_j} J(\theta) &= \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta_j} \frac{1}{m} Cost(h_\theta(x^{(i)}, y^{(i)}) \\ &= \dots \\ &= (h_\theta(x) - y) x_j \\ \end{align} $$

  • So we have the logistic regression algorithm:

    1. start with some initial $ \theta $
    2. repeatedly performs $ \theta_j := \theta_j - \alpha \frac{\partial{}}{\partial{\theta_j}} J(\theta) = \theta_j - \alpha (h_\theta(x^{(i)}) - y^{(i)}) x_j^{(i)} $ until convergence
    3. $ \alpha $ is called the learning rate

Multi-class classification: One-vs-all

  1. Train a logistic regression classifier $ h_\theta^{(i)}(x) $ for each class $ i $ to predict the probability that $ y = i $.

  2. On a new input $ x $, to make a prediction, pick the class $ i $ that maximizes $ h_\theta^{(i)}(x) $
